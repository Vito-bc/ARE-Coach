# NYC Zoning — Floor Area Ratio (FAR)
Floor Area Ratio (FAR) is the ratio of a building's total floor area to the area of its zoning
lot. Each zoning district sets a maximum FAR that limits how much floor area may be built. FAR
controls the overall bulk of a building, not its footprint alone; two buildings with the same
FAR can have very different footprints and heights.

# NYC — Certificate of Occupancy
A Certificate of Occupancy (CO) is issued by the NYC Department of Buildings (DOB) and states a
building's legal use and occupancy. No new building may be lawfully occupied without a valid CO.
A Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO) may be issued when a building or space is safe to
occupy but not yet fully complete; TCOs are time-limited and must be renewed.

# NYC Landmarks Preservation Commission — Approvals
The NYC Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) reviews proposed changes to designated landmarks.
A Certificate of No Effect (CNE) applies when work requires a DOB permit but does not affect
protected architectural features. A Permit for Minor Work (PMW) covers minor work that does not
require a DOB permit. A Certificate of Appropriateness (CofA) is required for significant
alterations that affect protected features and requires a public hearing before the Commission.

# NYC Building Code — Special Inspections
The NYC Building Code (based on the IBC) requires special inspections by a qualified agency for
certain construction work, such as concrete, structural steel, masonry, and firestopping. Special
inspections are distinct from DOB progress inspections, and the required items are listed in the
code's special-inspection provisions and the approved statement of special inspections.

# NYC — Construction Classification and Occupancy
Like the IBC, the NYC Building Code classifies buildings by construction type (based on the fire
resistance of structural elements) and by occupancy group (based on use). Together these determine
allowable height and area, required fire separations, and egress requirements.
